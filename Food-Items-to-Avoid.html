9 Food Items to Avoid Like the Plague if You Want to Have Great Teeth

A healthy diet is essential to maintain good oral health, and the teeth need special care to avoid decay, gum infections, and other health problems. And therefore, you should not increase the number of foods that harm their health. Sugar is not the only factor that breaks teeth, and many other foods can cause decay, erode teeth, and other problems. We mention the following:

citrus fruits

It is better to lack the intake of acidic foods and acidic drinks, as they harm the health of tooth enamel and cause tooth erosion. It increases the risk of developing decay, and it causes discomfort when mouth sores are present among the most prominent acidic foods that cause tooth damage: lemon and oranges, whether they are eaten as a fruit or juice, in addition to tomatoes.

Ice cream

Although ice does not contain any substances except water after freezing it, it can harm the teeth, and ice cream and others contain sugars that are harmful to the teeth, as ice cream causes tooth sensitivity, and chewing solids causes damage to the enamel.

Coffee and tea

Excessive caffeine consumption can affect Like coffee and tea, it negatively affects dental health and increases the risk of necrosis because it causes dehydration in the oral cavity, which leads to a decrease in saliva production, and thus a condition called dry mouth, and caffeine drinks usually contain added sugar; In addition, consuming these drinks excessively can lead to staining of the teeth, and to reduce their harmful effects, it is recommended to drink a large amount of water afterward.

Gelatin dessert

As it adheres to the teeth and the sugar and the resulting acids remain for a long time, which negatively affects the enamel layer. Still, in the event that it is eaten, it is recommended that it be part of the meal and not as a separate snack, as this helps to wash the mouth of sweets and acid due to the presence of a more significant amount of saliva during meals.

Fruit juice

Although fruit juice provides a large number of vitamins and antioxidants, it also contains a high percentage of sugar, and it may sometimes reach the same rate of sugar found in soft drinks, and therefore. It is recommended to consume juices without adding sugar, as fruits taste Naturally sweet, you can also add water to reduce the sugar content.

Sugars

Sugar attracts harmful bacteria. This leads to damage to tooth enamel and thus can cause decay of infected teeth and the formation of cavities, so it is advised to reduce the consumption of foods and drinks rich in sugar and to avoid consuming them between meals and directly before bed in particular, and if you want to eat them, it is preferable to take them with meals; During eating, the mouth produces more saliva, which helps to moisturize food pieces inside the mouth and reduce the effect of acid production.

Tooth Rinse

Many people are accustomed to using dental care products such as tooth rinse to get rid of food residues between the teeth or to get a refreshing breath. Still, excessive use of dental care products too often causes erosion of the tooth enamel layer, which leads to a feeling of tooth sensitivity. Hence, the layer of tooth enamel works to protect the teeth from pain when consuming very cold or hot drinks, so use dental care products regularly and without excessive use to take care of your teeth.

Soft Drinks

When you drink soft drinks for long periods, plaque bacteria use this sugar to produce acids that attack your enamel, and they also cause dry mouth. And because most soft drinks, including sugar-free ones, contain acids and are therefore harmful to the teeth.

Salty Biscuit

It is surprising to know that salty biscuits are almost the most dangerous food for oral health, so the carbohydrates in them are quickly broken down in the mouth, and within seconds they become just a lump of sugar on which pathogenic bacteria feed on and the bacteria while feeding on sugars secrete acid on the teeth, which may lead to the deposition of dental plaque Tooth decay.

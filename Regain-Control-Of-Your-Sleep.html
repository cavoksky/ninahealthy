Regain Control Of Your Sleep naturally

Sleeping well has a strong effect on our mental and physical health. If you don‚Äôt get enough sleep, it can harm your daytime energy, productivity, emotional balance, and even your weight. Despite this, many of us keep on tossing and turning at night, unable to get enough sleep.

If you get a hard time trying to sleep quite often, then there is a strong chance that you may have insomnia;

Chronic insomnia is typically caused by stress, life events, or sleep-disturbing habits. Insomnia can be resolved by treating the underlying cause, but it can sometimes last for years.

What are the causes?

Chronic insomnia is caused by a variety of factors, including:

Stress: Work, school, health, finances, or family concerns can keep your mind active at night, making it difficult to sleep. Insomnia can also be caused by stressful life events or trauma, such as the death or illness of a loved one, failure, divorce, or job loss.

Travel or work schedule: Your circadian rhythms function as an internal clock, guiding your sleep-wake cycle, metabolism, and body temperature. Insomnia can be caused by disrupting your body‚Äôs circadian rhythms. Jet lag from traveling across multiple time zones, working a late or early shift, or frequently changing shifts are possible causes.

Irregular bedtime schedule, naps, stimulating activities before bed, an uncomfortable sleep environment, and using your bed for work, eating, or watching TV are examples of poor sleep habits. Computers, televisions, video games, smartphones, and other screens just before bedtime can disrupt your sleep cycle.

How to regain control back of your sleep naturally?

You don‚Äôt have to live on drugs to get a good night‚Äôs sleep, but if you follow these steps, you will notice an improvement in the pattern and length of your sleep!
Here are few tips on how to do so :

Try to sleep and wake up at the same time every day


This aids in setting your body‚Äôs internal clock and improves the quality of your sleep. Choose a bedtime when you are normally tired to avoid tossing and turning. If you get enough sleep, you will wake up naturally without the use of an alarm clock.

Avoid sleeping in on weekends


if your weekend and weekday sleep schedules vary, the worse your jetlag-like symptoms will be. If you must make up for a late-night, take a nap during the day rather than sleeping in. This enables you to repay your sleep debt without disrupting your natural sleep-wake cycle.

Be cautious about napping


While napping is a good way to catch up on lost sleep, if you have trouble falling or staying asleep at night, napping can exacerbate the problem. Naps should be limited to 15 to 20 minutes in the early afternoon.

Fight post-dinner drowsiness


If you start feeling tired and sleepy before bedtime, get up and do something stimulating, such as planning your next day, washing the dishes, calling a friend, or getting your clothes ready for the next day. If you succumb to drowsiness, you may wake up later in the night and have difficulty falling back asleep.

In the morning, expose yourself to direct sunlight, The closer you are to your wake-up time, the better.
Take your coffee outside, or eat breakfast in front of a sunny window, The light on your face will assist you in waking up.

Allow as much natural light as possible into your home or workspace. Keep the blinds and curtains open and try to move your desk closer to the window.

Use a light therapy box if necessary. This simulates sunlight and is particularly useful during the short winter days.

Avoid using bright screens within 1-2 hours of going to bed. The blue light emitted by devices such as the phone, tablet, computer, or television is particularly bothersome, You can reduce the impact by using smaller-screen devices, lowering the brightness, or using light-altering software such as f.lux.

Avoid watching late-night television, Not only does TV light suppress melatonin production, but many programs are stimulating rather than relaxing, Instead, listen to music or audiobooks.

Avoid reading with backlit devices, Backlit tablets are more disruptive than e-readers without their light source.

When it‚Äôs time to sleep, keep the room dark. To block light from windows, use heavy curtains or shades, or try a sleep mask. Consider covering up any light-emitting electronics as well.

If you wake up in the middle of the night, turn off the lights. If you need light to move around safely, install a dim nightlight in the hall or get a small flashlight.

Those who exercise regularly sleep better at night and feel less sleepy during the day.
The strongly you exercise, the more sleep benefits you will get.
However, even light exercise, such as walking for 10 minutes every day, improves sleep quality.
It may take several months of consistent activity before you feel the full sleep-promoting benefits. So be patient and concentrate on developing a lasting exercise habit.

Reduce your intake of caffeine and nicotine. You might be shocked to know that caffeine can interfere with sleep for up to ten to twelve hours after consumption! Similarly, smoking is a stimulant that can interfere with your sleep, particularly if you smoke close to bedtime.

Avoid eating large meals at night. Dinner should be served earlier in the evening, and heavy, rich foods should be avoided within two hours of going to bed. Spicy or acidic foods can upset the stomach and cause heartburn.

Avoid alcohol before going to bed. While a nightcap may aid you to relax, it disrupts your sleep cycle once you‚Äôre awake.

Avoid drinking excessive amounts of liquids in the evening. Drinking a lot of fluids may necessitate frequent bathroom visits throughout the night.

Reduce your intake of sugary foods and refined carbohydrates. Eating many sugar and refined carbs during the day, such as white bread, white rice, and pasta, can cause you to wake up at night and pull you out of the deep, restorative stages of sleep.
